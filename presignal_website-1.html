Finding 01 — Conductivity
38% of Grand River sites run above safe conductivity limits — permanently
The worst site records a mean of 1,522 µS/cm — more than twice the 700 µS/cm standard — with spikes to 10,069 µS/cm (14× the limit). This drives calcium carbonate scaling on heat exchanger surfaces, reducing cooling efficiency and triggering thermal throttling in high-density GPU racks.
Finding 02 — Turbidity
95% of Grand River sites exceed turbidity standards. The watershed average is 6× the limit.
Mean turbidity across the watershed: 23 FNU. Standard: 4.0 FNU. City of Brantford's own intake records confirm it: Grand River raw water averaged 5.6–9.0 NTU monthly from 2021 to 2026. Suspended solids accumulate in cooling loops, fouling heat exchangers and providing substrate for biofilm.
Finding 03 — The Blind Spot
Standard threshold alarms miss chronic baseline exceedances entirely
Water treatment systems alarm when readings cross a threshold. But if the source water is already above threshold before it enters the system, the alarm never triggers — it's already in the "normal" zone for that facility. The Grand River operates in a state of chronic exceedance that reactive monitoring is structurally blind to.
Finding 04 — The Solution
A 60-year seasonal baseline detects drift before thresholds are crossed
The Presignal Subtraction Framework evaluates each new reading against 60 years of seasonal baseline data specific to that station. Anomalies are flagged when readings deviate from historical norms — not when they exceed static thresholds. This provides a predictive lead time that threshold monitoring cannot offer.

About Presignal
Source Water Intelligence · Niagara Region, Ontario
What This Is
Presignal is a source water intelligence project that identified a gap nobody was looking at: the connection between chronic water quality exceedances in Ontario's Grand River watershed and the cooling infrastructure risk those exceedances create for the AI data center cluster being built in Cambridge and Brantford.

This dashboard is the public-facing layer of that work. The underlying data is 60 years of Ontario Provincial Water Quality Monitoring Network (PWQMN) measurements — 7.6 million individual readings across 2,128 monitoring stations — processed through the Presignal Subtraction Framework to establish per-parameter seasonal baselines and identify chronic exceedances of Ontario operational standards.
The Presignal Subtraction Framework
Derived from geophysical signal analysis methodology developed for the Geological Pathway Diversity Model (GPDM) — a framework for classifying anomalous luminous phenomena — the Subtraction Framework classifies water quality observations into four categories:

Category I — Prosaic: Within expected seasonal variation
Category II — Concealed: Systematic drift below detection thresholds
Category III — Industry Artifact: Treatment intervention creating false-normal readings
Category IV — Genuine Anomaly: Statistically significant deviation from historical baseline

Grand River source water exceedances are classified as Category II — chronic baseline deviation that reactive monitoring is structurally unable to detect.
