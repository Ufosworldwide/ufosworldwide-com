## CHAPTER 1 — The Philosophy

Most people use AI video tools like a vending machine. They type something vague, get something mediocre, and assume the tool is limited.

The tool is not limited. The input is.

Hollywood didn't get good because of cameras. It got good because of language — the specific, shared vocabulary that lets a director tell a cinematographer exactly what they mean. When a director says "give me a Kubrick Stare with a Fincher push," everyone on set knows what to do.

That's what this book gives you. The language.

Once you have the language, the AI becomes a crew of a thousand people who have watched every film ever made and can execute any instruction instantly, for free.

**The core rule:** Be a director, not a wisher. Directors don't say "make it look cool." They say "85mm lens, shallow depth of field, subject lit from the left with one practical behind them, slow push in over 8 seconds as she reads the letter." That specificity is what separates forgettable from cinematic.

**Three things every great prompt has:**

- A camera instruction (lens, distance, movement)
- A lighting instruction (source, quality, direction)
- An emotional instruction (what should the viewer feel)

Everything else is detail layered on top.

### FOUNDATIONAL SHOTS — All Genres

**1. THE KUBRICK STARE**
Low angle. Subject's face tilted slightly down, eyes looking up at camera. Unsettling, powerful, slightly menacing.
*APEX-CAM: 24mm, 3ft distance, 15° below eye line.*

**2. THE SPIELBERG FACE LIGHT**
Close-up on a face reacting to something wondrous or horrifying. Key light bouncing in the eyes. Pure emotion.
*APEX-CAM: 85mm, soft front bounce at iris level.*

**3. THE FINCHER SLOW PUSH**
Methodical dolly-in during dialogue or revelation. Slow. Deliberate. You feel it before you see it.
*APEX-CAM: 35mm, 0.5 mph push, starting at 8ft.*

**4. THE SCORSESE STEADICAM FOLLOW**
Fluid, continuous tracking behind or alongside a character moving through space. Energy without chaos.
*APEX-CAM: 35mm, body-mounted stabilized, 3ft behind subject.*

**5. THE DEAKINS SILHOUETTE**
Subject backlit against bright sky or window. Shape only. Mood over detail.
*APEX-CAM: 50mm, expose for background, subject in shadow.*

**6. THE DUTCH ANGLE**
Camera tilted 15–45 degrees. Psychological unease. Something is wrong.
*APEX-CAM: Any lens, 20–30° rotation.*

**7. THE OVERHEAD GOD SHOT**
Directly overhead looking down. Characters become patterns. Scale shifts.
*APEX-CAM: 24mm, directly above, 20–40ft up.*

**8. THE EXTREME CLOSE-UP INSERT**
Eyes. Hands. A ring. A trigger. One detail that carries everything.
*APEX-CAM: 135mm macro, 6 inches from subject.*

**9. THE EPIC WIDE ESTABLISHING**
Full environment. Character small in a large world. Scale is the message.

**10. THE OVER-SHOULDER**
Looking past one character at another. Conversation, confrontation, connection.

### Standard Coverage Formula

Every scene needs coverage. Here's the minimum:

1. Wide establishing (Shot 9) — orient the viewer
2. Medium two-shot (Shot 14) — establish the relationship
3. Over-shoulder reversal (Shot 10 + 12) — the conversation
4. Close-up reactions (Shot 2 + 15) — the emotion
5. Insert detail (Shot 8) — what matters most


### Prompt Length Rule

Keep prompts under 2,000 characters. Over that and AI video tools start ignoring the later instructions. Front-load what matters most.

**Priority order:**
1. Shot type and camera
2. Subject and action
3. Lighting
4. Mood
5. Style/color

## CHAPTER 6 — Continuity Locks

When you're building a multi-clip sequence — a fight, a chase, a story — each prompt needs to hand off to the next. This is called a Continuity Lock.

At the end of every prompt (except the last), add a CONTINUITY LOCK section that specifies exactly what state the next prompt must begin in.

---

### Continuity Lock Template

```
CONTINUITY LOCK — [Scene Name] Prompt [N] to [N+1]:
- Subject position: [where they are, facing what direction]
- Lighting state: [current lighting, time of day]
- Environment state: [what damage, change, or progression has occurred]
- Character appearance: [clothing, wounds, emotional state]
- Action bridge: [final action of this clip = opening action of next]
```

---

### Example — Four-Prompt Action Sequence

**Prompt 1 ends with:**
```
CONTINUITY LOCK — Battle Sequence Prompt 1 to 2:
Subject position: standing center of intersection,
facing north, surrounded on three sides.
Lighting: golden hour, sun at 20° above horizon, west.
Environment: two vehicles overturned, debris field
spreading 30ft radius. Character: jacket torn left
shoulder, no visible wounds yet.
Action bridge: protagonist raises weapon as
the three attackers converge simultaneously.
```

**Prompt 2 begins with:**
```
Continuing directly from previous clip —
protagonist weapon raised, three attackers
converging from north, east, and south.
Golden hour lighting, west sun low.
Two overturned vehicles visible in background.
```

This is how you get a coherent four-minute film out of four separate AI clips.

---

### Split-Screen Rule

AI video tools struggle with more than 3 simultaneous frames. If you want split-screen effects:

- Maximum 3 panels at once
- Give each panel its own line in the prompt
- Label them: TOP THIRD / MIDDLE THIRD / BOTTOM THIRD

### Claymation / Stop-Motion

Add at the top of every prompt:
```
Claymation stop-motion animation, hand-animated clay
figures, each frame photographed individually,
24fps playback from individual photographs,
tactile texture, visible fingerprints and imperfections
in the clay — this is the aesthetic, not a flaw.
```

Scale the APEX-CAM down:
```
APEX-CAM™: 24mm macro lens positioned 0.5 inches
from set surface. Every fiber, texture, and clay
surface rendered at maximum detail.
```

**Key claymation prompt notes:**
- Describe characters as "clay figures" with specific physical features
- Reference scale explicitly — "carpet fibers like a prehistoric forest"
- Use "volumetric light rays" and "practical amber lighting" for under-world atmospheres
- Add "Cooke Anamorphic macro glass" to the camera spec

---

### Anime — Hand-Drawn Traditional Style

Add to top of prompt:
```
Hand-drawn Japanese anime, cel animation style,
traditional ink outlines, flat color fills with
subtle shading, 24fps with intentional 2s timing
(animated on twos for classic anime feel).
```

**Vintage anime variant:**
```
1960s–1970s vintage hand-drawn anime aesthetic,
aged film grain, slightly faded color palette,
dramatic speed lines, exaggerated action poses,
classic Toei Animation composition style.
```

**Genre mixing for anime:**
- Ninja/martial arts: add "dynamic speed lines, impact frames, dramatic pause before strike"
- Mecha: add "mechanical detail in joints, exhaust vapor, weight and mass implied through movement"
- Slice of life: add "soft backgrounds, detailed everyday objects, gentle lighting, emotional restraint"

---

### Kaiju / Live-Action Monster Scale

For large-scale destruction sequences:
```
Cinematic live-action kaiju style, 9:16 vertical,
practical miniature city models with pyrotechnic
destruction, Toho Studios techniques,
scale established through foreground human elements.
```

**Scale anchors** — always include a human-scale reference:
- "Ships tossed aside like toys"
- "Cars crushed underfoot like paper"
- "People on the street the size of ants from this angle"

**Destruction progression language:**
- Prompt 1: "first contact, city intact, creature emerging"
- Prompt 2: "mid-battle, 30% urban destruction, fires starting"
- Prompt 3: "climax, 70% destruction, smoke everywhere, orange sky"
- Prompt 4: "aftermath, silence, dawn light, scale of loss visible"

## CHAPTER 8 — The Music Framework

Suno is a music generation AI that responds to the same principle as video tools: specificity wins. Vague inputs produce generic outputs. Precise inputs produce music that sounds like it came from a real producer with a clear vision.

---

### The Suno Prompt Formula

```
[GENRE] + [SUBGENRE] + [TEMPO/BPM] + [KEY INSTRUMENTS] +
[MOOD DESCRIPTORS] + [REFERENCE ARTISTS] + [STRUCTURE TAGS]
```

**Always include:**
- A BPM range (not optional — this anchors everything)
- At least 2 reference artists (gives the AI a sonic target)
- At least 3 mood/texture words
- The key instrumentation you want

---

### Structure Tags for Suno

Use these inside square brackets to control song architecture:

```
[Intro - 8 bars]
[Verse 1]
[Pre-Chorus]
[Chorus]
[Verse 2]
[Bridge]
[Breakdown]
[Final Drop]
[Outro - fade]
```

For EDM/dance formats, repeat the chorus tag to force the hypnotic loop structure:

```
[Chorus]
[Chorus - peak energy]
[Chorus - maximum]
```

## CHAPTER 10 — Voice & Audio Production

When Suno won't do what you need — spoken word, phonetics, custom narration — here's the two-tool workflow.

---

### ElevenLabs — Voice Generation

ElevenLabs is the best AI voice tool available. Use it for:
- Narration and voiceover
- Character voices
- Phonetic sound banks for apps
- Any audio where the voice is the product

**Voice settings for different uses:**

| Use | Stability | Clarity | Style |
|---|---|---|---|
| Documentary narration | 70% | 80% | 0% |
| Character emotional | 40% | 75% | 60% |
| Spirit box / EVP bank | 50% | 85% | 0% |
| Audiobook | 65% | 80% | 20% |

**For phonetic/EVP word banks:**

Type your phonemes separated by periods for pacing:
```
ah. ay. ee. eye. oh. oo. uh. air. ear.
bah. pah. dah. tah. gah. kah. fah. vah.
ma. me. mi. mo. mu. na. ne. ni. no. nu.
```

The period forces ElevenLabs to treat each sound as discrete rather than running them together.

---

### Audacity — Layer and Mix

Once you have your ElevenLabs audio, open Audacity to add:

**Adding white noise as a second layer:**
1. Generate → Noise → White, Amplitude 0.05
2. Place on Track 2 below your voice
3. Voice should be clearly audible over noise — adjust amplitude if needed
4. Tracks menu → Mix and Render
5. Export → MP3, 192kbps

**For spirit box / paranormal audio:**
- White noise amplitude: 0.03–0.08 (lower = more subtle)
- Add a second Audacity effect: "Reverb" with 15% room size
- Export at 128kbps — slight compression artifacts add authenticity

### When Suno Won't Cooperate — Spoken Word Workaround

Suno reads everything as music. To get spoken word or phonetic tracks out of it:

**Method 1 — Instrumental tag:**
```
[Instrumental]
white noise generator, static background,
disembodied voice samples clearly speaking: [phonemes],
no music whatsoever, paranormal EVP recording,
spoken phonemes, radio static texture, field recording
```

**Method 2 — Found audio framing:**
```
[Instrumental]
found audio recording, spirit box session,
white noise with fragmented voice saying [phonemes],
degraded audio quality, radio interference,
no musical elements, raw paranormal evidence
```

**Method 3 — Nuclear option:**
If it still adds music, add all of these:
- "no instruments no drums no bass no melody"
- "acapella" (even though it's not singing)
- "field recording"
- "voice sample only"
- "spoken word sample"

**Best solution:** Use ElevenLabs + Audacity. Suno was built for music. Don't fight it for spoken word.

## CHAPTER 11 — The Portrait Vision Framework

Generating beautiful, specific, emotionally true human beings in AI image tools.

---

### The Master Formula

```
[STYLE] portrait of a human being.

SUBJECT: [age range, features, skin tone, hair,
expression, what their eyes say, body language]

SETTING: [where are they exactly]

LIGHTING: [source, quality, direction, temperature]

MOOD: [one or two words]

FRAMING: [how the camera sees them]

QUALITY: photorealistic, high detail, shallow depth
of field, shot on 85mm lens, professional portrait
photography, no AI artifacts
```

---

### Style Menu

- Photorealistic, like a high-end magazine shoot
- Cinematic, like a still from a film
- Painterly, like an oil portrait with texture
- Editorial fashion, clean and deliberate
- Fine art portrait, museum-quality stillness
- Documentary real, unposed and honest
- Soft film grain, vintage warmth

---

### Subject Library — Ready-to-Use Descriptions

**The woman who has decided something:**
A woman in her 40s with deep-set dark eyes and silver-streaked locs, jaw set like she's made a choice and isn't explaining it to anyone.

**The young man carrying too much:**
A man in his mid-20s with warm golden-brown skin, soft curls, eyes that look like they've seen too much too young.

**The elder with history in her face:**
A woman in her late 70s, laugh lines deep, white hair loose, hands folded like a prayer she's said ten thousand times.

**The observer:**
A girl mid-teens, freckled, head tilted slightly, the specific expression of someone who notices everything and says very little.

**The one who doesn't ask for approval:**
A woman with a shaved head, strong jaw, bold features, direct gaze. She is not asking for your approval and the frame knows it.

**The ancient face:**
Non-binary person, soft and angular at once, expression that reads like something older than the photograph — this face has always existed somewhere.

---

### Lighting Menu

- Golden hour — warm, low, sideways light that makes skin glow from inside
- Dramatic single side shadow — one half lit, one half dark, the whole story in the contrast
- Soft north window light — even, cool, the light painters used before electricity existed
- Candlelight — orange and intimate, shadows moving at the edges
- Blue hour — the last light before dark, everything slightly purple and unreal
- Overcast natural — flat, honest, nowhere to hide
- Backlit silhouette — the shape against the brightest part of the sky
- Neon reflected — urban, cool, electric color on skin

---

### Setting Menu

- Standing at a rain-wet window, city blur behind her
- A field at the edge of a forest, golden and overgrown
- An empty kitchen at 2am, one light over the stove
- A rooftop at dusk, the city dropping away below
- An old church, light falling through stained glass
- A market in full motion — she is still while everything moves
- White studio: nothing but the person
- Chest-deep in still water at dawn, mist around her
- A back porch in summer, nothing special, which makes it everything

---

### Mood Menu

- Tender — like something delicate is being held
- Defiant — she is still here and that is the whole statement
- Contemplative — mid-thought, somewhere internal
- Joyful but not performing it — real laughter, not posed
- Grieving, but upright
- Peaceful in a way that took a long time to earn
- The moment just before she speaks
- Something ancient — like this face has always existed

---

### Framing Menu

- Extreme close-up on the eyes only — the whole story lives there
- Close crop from collar to crown — face fills the frame
- Waist-up, hands visible — what the hands do matters
- Full body, standing, surrounded by environment
- Side profile, sharp — the architecture of the face
- From behind, looking out at something we can't see
- Low angle looking up — weight and presence
- High angle looking down — vulnerability, tenderness
- Environmental wide — the person small in a large world

---

### Platform Notes

**Canva Dream Lab / Magic Media:**
Use the full formula. Select 4:5 portrait ratio. Generate in batches of 4 and pick the best. Canva responds well to emotional language — lean into mood words.

**MidJourney:**
Append to end of every prompt:
`--ar 2:3 --style raw --v 6.1 --q 2`

**Ideogram:**
Select: Realistic · HD · Portrait 4:5
Ideogram is the best of the three for skin tone accuracy and emotional expression in faces. Use it when the person's face is the whole point.

## CHAPTER 12 — Putting Audio, Video & Image Together

Here is the complete workflow for taking a creative idea from concept to finished multi-media output.

---

### The Pipeline

```
CONCEPT
    ↓
IMAGE — Generate the visual world and characters first.
Use Portrait Vision Framework. Lock the look.
    ↓
MUSIC — Generate the emotional soundtrack.
Use Suno with the genre blueprint that matches the tone.
    ↓
VIDEO — Generate the moving image.
Use APEX-CAM + Shot Library + Genre Mixing Guide.
Build in a 4-prompt sequence with Continuity Locks.
    ↓
VOICE — Add narration or dialogue if needed.
ElevenLabs → Audacity mix.
    ↓
ASSEMBLE — Combine in any video editor.
CapCut, DaVinci Resolve, iMovie all work.
```

---

### The One-Day Short Film Workflow

**Morning — Visual Bible (2 hours)**
- Generate 3–5 portrait images of your main characters using the Portrait Framework
- Generate 3–5 setting images using the Setting Menu
- Pick the ones that feel like your world. Lock them.

**Midday — Music (1 hour)**
- Choose your genre blueprint
- Generate 3–4 tracks in Suno
- Pick the one that matches your film's emotional register
- Keep the others as alt versions

**Afternoon — Video (3–4 hours)**
- Write your 4-prompt video sequence using the formula
- Include Continuity Locks between each prompt
- Generate in Sora, Runway, or Kling
- Regenerate any clip that breaks continuity

**Evening — Assembly (1–2 hours)**
- Drop clips in order in CapCut
- Layer your Suno track underneath
- Add any ElevenLabs narration on a third track
- Color grade: most tools have a "cinematic" preset — use it
- Export at highest available resolution

---

### Troubleshooting

**Video clips don't match each other:**
Your Continuity Lock wasn't specific enough. Add: exact lighting state, character position, and a one-sentence action bridge.

**Suno keeps adding instruments when you want voice only:**
Switch to ElevenLabs + Audacity. Don't fight the tool.

**Faces in images look AI:**
Add to prompt: "photorealistic, no AI artifacts, shot on film, slight grain, not CGI." In MidJourney: use --style raw.

**Video prompt is generating the wrong thing:**
You front-loaded the wrong information. The AI reads left to right and weighs the beginning of the prompt heaviest. Put your most important instruction first.

**Clips are too short:**
AI video tools default to short clips. Add "extended scene, minimum 8 seconds, slow pacing, no jump cuts" to your prompt.
